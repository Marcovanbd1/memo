WORLD EXCLUSIVE · THE OPENING

Inside the
Stone That
Remembers

On the morning MEMO Portland opened its tunnels to the world, I pressed my palms against 170 million years of silence and felt something I cannot fully name.

Mark Winterburn
Travel · Philosophy · History
Portland, Dorset · 14 September 2028

The queue formed before dawn. Not because anyone had been asked to arrive early, but because people simply couldn't stay away. By five in the morning there were perhaps three hundred of us standing on the eastern face of the island, the sea invisible below in the September dark, the stone above us ancient and indifferent. Someone had brought a flask of tea. Someone else had brought their grandmother. A school group from Nottingham stood in a line of high-visibility jackets, their teacher holding a clipboard she never once consulted. We were all, in our way, waiting for something we could not quite describe.

I had been here before, of course. I grew up on this island, or close enough. As a child I moved to Portland from London, where my weekends had been spent among the monuments of the city — the dome of St Paul's most of all, that great pale canopy of Portland stone rising above the Thames. I loved the stone. I didn't know then that I was loving Portland at a distance, that the same geological body that held Wren's cathedral had been quarried from the cliffs I would one day walk. When I arrived on the island, aged ten, I stopped loving stone abstractly. I played in the quarries. I ran my hands along the cool faces of the cuts. I felt, without understanding it, that the stone was holding something.

What MEMO has taken twenty years to build, and what Sebastian Brooke spent two decades believing was possible when almost no one else did, is the answer to that childish intuition. The stone is holding something. It is holding time. Not the ordinary kind — not the decades measured in human ambition or municipal budgets — but the other kind. The kind that swallows civilisations without noticing.

The tunnels opened at seven. The light at the entrance was the colour of old honey, warm LEDs set into the stone at ankle height so that the ceiling remained in shadow. Sixty feet above, somewhere, was the sky. Down here there was only the smell of limestone and cold air and the faint sound of the sea pressing against the island's base. Eight miles of tunnel. Hundreds of millions of years of record. You do not enter MEMO. You are received by it.

I was in the second group through. The guide — a young man from Portland named Joel, who has been working on the excavation since he was seventeen — said almost nothing for the first few minutes. He simply walked, and we followed, and the tunnel narrowed and widened and narrowed again, and the stone on either side changed colour in ways that took your eyes a moment to register. Cream and grey and a faint reddish-brown where the iron had oxidised over geological time. On certain faces you could see the layering: strata as clear as the pages of a book left in the rain, each one a different chapter of the same enormous silence.

At the first chamber, Joel stopped. He raised his hand and the group fell quiet. In the wall to our left, at head height, there was a single ammonite the size of a dinner plate, its spiral cut cleanly through by the quarryman's blade, probably in the early nineteenth century. Whoever quarried it never paused. For them it was an obstacle, an irregularity in the face to be negotiated. For us, standing in the dark two hundred years later, it was an eye. A coiled eye, looking back from 170 million years ago, utterly unconcerned.

Joel said: "You can touch it."

· · ·

I pressed both palms flat against the stone and held them there. The temperature was constant, around eleven degrees, the same it has been for as long as the tunnel has existed. The rock does not warm to your touch. It accepts it without responding. This is not coldness. It is something closer to honesty. You are the warm thing, the brief thing, the thing that arrived very recently and will leave very soon. The stone is simply being what it is.

I thought about the word extinction. I had been thinking about it for months, ever since the opening was announced, ever since I began to understand what MEMO was actually proposing. Not a museum of dead things. Not a cabinet of curiosities. A reckoning. The premise of this place — stated plainly, without apology, on every wall of the first chamber — is that we are living through a mass extinction event. The sixth. The previous five were geological. Volcanic. Asteroidal. The work of deep time acting on itself. This one is different. This one has a face.

What MEMO does, with extraordinary restraint, is refuse to let you look away from that. The exhibits do not lecture. They simply place you in the presence of what has been lost and allow the stone around you to provide the context. The Portland limestone you are standing in contains the record of the Triassic-Jurassic extinction event, 201 million years ago, in which perhaps eighty percent of all species on Earth disappeared. You are, literally, inside a previous ending. And the designers of this place — Brooke and his team, twenty years in the making — understood that this was the argument. You do not need to be told that extinction is serious. You need to be inside it.

By nine o'clock the tunnels held perhaps four hundred people, and yet the silence held too. I have never been in a crowd that was collectively this quiet. Not hushed in the way of churches, where silence is a performance. Quiet in the way of people who have been stopped in their tracks by something larger than themselves. A woman next to me was crying without any apparent distress. She caught me looking and said, simply: "I didn't expect it to feel like this." I told her I knew what she meant. I wasn't sure I did, entirely. But I knew the feeling was real.

Near the end of the main tunnel, in the largest chamber, there is a single oak bench and a single spotlight and, on the wall in front of you, an installation that I will not fully describe because it should be encountered rather than anticipated. What I will say is that it lists — in white text on the uncut stone face — the names of species that have ceased to exist in the lifetime of every person currently alive. The list is very long. The font is very small. And the stone, cool and ancient and entirely indifferent, holds it all without comment.

I sat on the bench for a long time. Joel came and sat beside me, which I had not expected from a guide. He said he still comes in on his days off. He said the stone does something to you after a while. I asked him what. He thought about it. "It makes the panic go quiet," he said. "Not because it tells you it'll be alright. More like — it's been this bad before. Worse. And the stone's still here."

I wrote that down in my notebook and looked at it for a long time.

· · ·

Outside, re-entering the September morning, the sky was very blue and the sea was visible now and Weymouth sat in its gentle arc across the water, its buildings catching the light. The queue was still forming below. Someone had set up a small stall selling hot drinks. There were children running on the grass above the tunnel entrance, careless and brilliant in the sun.

I had been underground for three hours. I felt, as I stood in the light, the particular quality of gratitude that comes from being returned to the ordinary world with new instructions. The instructions were not written down anywhere. They were simply in the stone, and now they were in me, and I would have to work out what to do with them.

MEMO Portland is not a visitor centre. It is not a museum, not in any sense that word has previously meant. It is a conversation with deep time, conducted in the dark, inside the island that has always held the record. Sebastian Brooke spent twenty years believing this was possible. Standing in the morning light above the tunnels on the day it opened, I thought: he was right to believe it. And I thought: the world has no idea yet what it has been given.

It will find out.
